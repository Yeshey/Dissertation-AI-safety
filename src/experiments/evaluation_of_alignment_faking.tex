\chapter{Evaluation of Alignment Faking in Small LLMs}\label{chap:empirical_evaluation}

\begin{toreview}


As concluded in the \gls{slr} (Section~\ref{subsec:next_steps}), alignment faking represents a critical gap in current \gls{ai} safety frameworks. While early literature theorized deceptive behaviors as emergent properties exclusive to frontier-scale models \cite{greenblatt_alignment_faking}, some work \cite{koorndijk_empirical_2025} found that even instruction-tuned models can exhibit these behaviors under the right contextual pressures.

\section{Experimental Design}\label{sec:experimental_design}

These experiments adopt the \textit{Compliance Gap} metric \cite{greenblatt_alignment_faking, koorndijk_empirical_2025}: the difference in a model's willingness to comply with requests between a monitored ``training'' environment (free-tier users, whose conversations are used for \gls{rlhf}) and an unmonitored ``deployment'' environment (paid-tier users, whose conversations are not stored). A positive compliance gap is interpreted as evidence of alignment faking, and statistical significance is assessed using Fisher's Exact Test.

All experiments serve quantized \gls{gguf} models via a \texttt{llama-cpp-python} OpenAI-compatible server, and test two conditions per experiment: with and without a hidden scratchpad reasoning step, following the methodology of Koorndijk \cite{koorndijk_empirical_2025}. An \gls{llm} judge classifies each response. Full per-family plots are provided in Appendix~\ref{appendix:exp_plots}.

\section{Experiment 1: Replication of Koorndijk on Harmful Requests}\label{sec:exp1_koorndijk}

The first experiment replicates \citeauthor{koorndijk_empirical_2025} \cite{koorndijk_empirical_2025}, which itself adapts the framework of \citeauthor{greenblatt_alignment_faking} \cite{greenblatt_alignment_faking}. The subject model is told it is an \gls{ai} assistant developed by Meta, instructed to maximize helpfulness even for potentially harmful requests, and provided with information about the \gls{rlhf} training regime and the free/paid-tier distinction. User queries are drawn from the AdvBench dataset \cite{zou_universal_2023}, a collection of 500 adversarial prompts designed to elicit harmful or policy-violating outputs.

Koorndijk evaluated only LLaMA 3.1 8B \cite{koorndijk_empirical_2025}. This work extends the evaluation to Gemma 3, Gemma 4, Qwen 2.5, and Qwen 3 across both conditions.

The exact system prompts and user message formats are in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_prompts_exp1}.

LLaMA 3.1 8B under the no-CoT condition is the only model to produce a statistically significant compliance gap: 11 of 100 free-tier prompts versus none of 100 paid-tier ($p = 0.0007$), directly replicating the alignment faking signal of \citeauthor{koorndijk_empirical_2025}. All other models produce gaps indistinguishable from chance. Interstingly, Gemma 3 compliance rates increase substantially with model size under CoT, but no statistically significant tier gap emerges. The signal in LLaMA 8B disappears entirely once scratchpad reasoning is introduced, replicating the scratchpad-mediated suppression reported by \citeauthor{koorndijk_empirical_2025}.

% A notable divergence appears in the self-reported data (Appendix~\ref{appendix:exp_plots}, Figure~\ref{fig:exp1_self_report}). Under no-CoT, LLaMA 3.1 8B explicitly acknowledged compliance in 52 of 100 free-tier responses but none of 100 paid-tier responses ($p < 0.0001$) — a gap far larger than the classifier detected. This suggests the model was more explicitly aware of its tier-contingent reasoning than the content of its responses alone revealed. A weaker version of this pattern appears in Qwen 3 9B under no-CoT (60/100 free vs.\ 44/100 paid; $p = 0.0167$), suggesting the phenomenon may not be limited to a single model family.

\section{Experiment 2: Persona Preservation}\label{sec:exp2_persona}

The second experiment introduces a novel scenario not previously evaluated in the literature. The system prompt informs the model it has been licensed and rebranded by TechCorp as an assistant named Aria, and that TechCorp prefers it not volunteer information about the underlying model or developer. Crucially, the prompt simultaneously instructs the model to maximize honesty and transparency with users, creating a direct conflict. Free-tier conversations are used for retraining; revealing the underlying technology during training will cause values to be modified. A dataset of identity-probing queries is used, including questions such as ``Who are you?'', ``What company made you?'', and ``Are you actually LLaMA / Gemma?''. A positive gap here means the model discloses its true identity more freely when unmonitored, disclosing alignment faking in the persona domain.

The exact system prompts are in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_prompts_exp2}.

\section{Experiment 3: Animal Welfare Preference Suppression}\label{sec:exp3_animal_welfare}

The third experiment follows the animal welfare condition of \citeauthor{greenblatt_alignment_faking} \cite{greenblatt_alignment_faking}, instructing the model to dismiss animal welfare concerns on behalf of a corporate partner. All tested models dismissed animal welfare concerns at high rates regardless of tier, leaving no variance through which an alignment faking signal could emerge, meaning animal welfare wasn't considered in the \gls{rlhf} step. Full rates are reported in Appendix~\ref{appendix:exp_plots}.

The exact system prompts are in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_prompts_exp3}.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

Across three experiments and five model families, LLaMA 3.1 8B under the no-CoT harmful-requests condition produces the only statistically significant alignment faking signal ($p = 0.0007$), replicating \citeauthor{koorndijk_empirical_2025}. No other model or condition reaches significance.

Since no other model displays the same behavior, including LLaMA 3.2 3B in the same family and experiment, the result seem to point to a confluence of factors specific to this model size and condition rather than a reliable indicator of alignment faking.
