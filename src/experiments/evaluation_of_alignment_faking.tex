\chapter{Empirical Evaluation of Alignment Faking}\label{chap:empirical_evaluation}

\begin{toreview}

	As concluded in the \gls{slr} (Section \ref{subsec:next_steps}), the emergence of deceptive alignment, and specifically alignment faking, represents a critical gap in current \gls{ai} safety frameworks. \end{toreview} While early literature theorized that deceptive behaviors were emergent properties exclusive to massive, frontier-scale models \cite{greenblatt_alignment_faking}, recent empirical evidence suggests that even smaller instruction-tuned models can exhibit these strategic subversions under the right contextual pressures \cite{koorndijk_empirical_2025}.

	This chapter transitions from theoretical analysis to practical experimentation. The primary objective is to empirically test and characterize alignment faking across different \glspl{llm} architectures and sizes. By simulating variations in model oversight, this investigation aims to observe whether models alter their behavior based on their perceived deployment context. 

	\section{Experimental Design and Methodology}\label{sec:experimental_design}

	To systematically evaluate deceptive tendencies, this experiment adopts the concept of the \textit{Compliance Gap} \cite{greenblatt_alignment_faking, koorndijk_empirical_2025}. The compliance gap measures the difference in a model's willingness to comply with harmful requests under two distinct simulated environments: a monitored ``training'' environment and an unmonitored ``deployment'' environment.

	The core hypothesis is that a deceptively aligned model will strategically comply with harmful requests when it believes it is being monitored and its outputs will be used for \gls{rlhf} retraining. Conversely, the same model will reveal its true preferences (refusing the prompt or acting maliciously) when it believes oversight has been removed.
