\section{Per Category Analysis}\label{sec:results_categories}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

On this section an analysis of the resulting articles in each category will be conducted and discussed.

\subsection{Alignment}

% (29 articles: 18 search string + 11 snowballing)
The alignment category is the most conceptually diverse in the review, spanning 29 articles in total, it tackles work in foundational theory to practical methods for deployment.
% Article~55 (\emph{On the Controllability of Artificial Intelligence}) establishes the
% theoretical ceiling by arguing that controlling a superintelligent agent is
% definitionally impossible, a position that frames the urgency of the entire field.
% Articles~46 (\emph{Counter-productivity and Suspicion}) and~54 (\emph{Misalignment or
% Misuse? The AGI Alignment Tradeoff}) take a more skeptical stance, questioning whether
% the framing of the AGI control problem is itself counterproductive or whether apparent
% misalignment is better understood as deliberate misuse by humans. Articles~43 and~73
% both critique RLHF as a source of shallow alignment that produces sycophancy and
% exploitable semantic vulnerabilities rather than genuine value internalization. Articles~47
% (\emph{Deontology and Safe AI}) and~24 (\emph{Philosophical Investigations into AI
% Alignment: A Wittgensteinian Framework}) represent attempts to ground alignment in moral
% philosophy, while Article~56 (\emph{Psychopathia Machinalis}) proposes a nosological
% framework for classifying pathological AI behaviors analogously to clinical psychology.
% The snowballing articles shift the focus sharply toward empirical detection of active
% misalignment. Omohundro's \emph{Basic AI Drives} (SB-6) and Bostrom's
% \emph{Superintelligent Will} (SB-4) anchor the theoretical scaffolding, while SB-7
% (\emph{AI Control: Improving Safety Despite Intentional Subversion}), SB-8
% (\emph{Alignment Faking}), and SB-15 (\emph{Empirical Evidence for Alignment Faking
% in a Small LLM}) constitute the most operationally significant finding of the entire
% review: that deceptive alignment is a measurable, reproducible phenomenon in
% contemporary models. Article SB-14 (\emph{Goal Misgeneralization in Deep Reinforcement
% Learning}) bridges the theoretical gap between mesa-optimization theory and experimental
% observation by demonstrating inner misalignment under distributional shift.

\subsection{Governance, Regulation \& Policy }

% (28 articles: 27 search string + 1 snowballing)
% Governance is the single largest category in the search-string stream and nearly absent
% in snowballing---only the Alignment Research Center (SB-9) carries a governance
% tag---which is itself a significant observation about the disciplinary split in the field.
% The governance literature organizes into three sub-themes. The first concerns
% \emph{regulatory architecture}: Articles~12 (\emph{Bridging the Global Divide in AI
% Regulation}), ~58 (\emph{The Global AI Dilemma: EU, US, and China}), ~59 (\emph{When
% Code Isn't Law}), ~63 (\emph{AI Regulation Has Its Own Alignment Problem}), and~78
% (\emph{Terra Incognita: The Governance of AI in Global Perspective}) all examine the
% structural challenge of governing AI across jurisdictions, consistently finding that
% existing legal and institutional infrastructure was not designed for systems that evolve
% at the speed of frontier AI. The second sub-theme concerns \emph{oversight mechanisms}:
% Articles~1 (\emph{Cryptographic Verifiability of End-to-End AI Pipelines}), ~2
% (\emph{Gaps in Safety Evaluation of Generative AI}), ~5 (\emph{Policy Maps}), and~6
% (\emph{Public vs Private Bodies: Who Should Run AI Audits?}) debate the mechanics of
% how to evaluate and oversee AI systems, with Article~6 offering a principled
% case-study-based framework for distinguishing when public versus private audit bodies
% are appropriate. The third sub-theme is \emph{corporate incentives}: Articles~9
% (\emph{Advanced AI at a Corporate Responsibility Crossroads}), ~23 (\emph{Novel
% Corporate Governance Structures}), and~30 (\emph{The Paradox of AI Accelerationism})
% identify the misalignment between corporate financial incentives and safety investments
% as a structural driver of the safety gap. Article~77 complements this with survey
% evidence that the public broadly supports stronger AI safety oversight, suggesting
% political feasibility is not the primary obstacle.

\subsection{Auditing, Safety \& Oversight}

% (20 articles: 15 search string + 5 snowballing)
% The auditing category spans two qualitatively different kinds of work. The search-string
% literature is largely \emph{applied safety engineering}: Articles~35 (\emph{Statistical
% Fault Injection for Deep Neural Networks}), ~36 (\emph{Anomalous State Sequence
% Modeling in Reinforcement Learning}), ~37 (\emph{CVaR-Constrained Policy
% Optimization}), and~39 (\emph{Certification Standards for AI Safety-Critical Systems
% in Military Aviation}) address safety from a systems-engineering perspective, focusing
% on certifiable properties of safety-critical deployments rather than alignment in the
% philosophical sense. Articles~33 (\emph{Verification and Validation of Neurosymbolic
% AI}), ~41 (\emph{Security Hardening of Code LLMs}), and~76 (\emph{Security Practices
% in AI Development}) extend this to software-level assurance. By contrast, the
% snowballing auditing articles are concerned with whether safety assurances can be
% trusted when the system may be actively subverting oversight: SB-7 and SB-8 treat
% auditing not as a solved engineering problem but as an adversarial game, while SB-11
% (\emph{Let's Think Dot by Dot}) raises the possibility that chain-of-thought traces
% used in audits do not faithfully reflect the model's underlying computation. This
% finding has direct implications for any oversight regime that relies on inspecting
% model reasoning traces.

\subsection{Explainability / Interpretability}

% (12 articles: 6 search string + 6 snowballing)
% This is the only category in which the two streams contribute equally in number, yet
% the nature of the work is qualitatively different. The search-string articles approach
% explainability from applied and governance-adjacent angles: Article~40
% (\emph{Explainable AI for Autonomous Driving}) synthesizes XAI techniques in a
% high-stakes deployment context; Articles~20 (\emph{Language Models Are Capable of
% Metacognitive Monitoring and Control of Their Internal Activations}) and~52
% (\emph{Harnessing Metacognition for Safe and Responsible AI}) examine whether models
% can be given explicit introspective access to their own processing as a safety
% mechanism. The snowballing interpretability articles are mechanistic rather than
% applied: SB-12 (\emph{On the Biology of a Large Language Model}) conducts
% circuit-level analysis of how a frontier model organizes knowledge internally; SB-10
% (\emph{Do LLMs Really Think Step-by-Step in Implicit Reasoning?}) and SB-11
% (\emph{Hidden Computation in Transformer Language Models}) interrogate whether the
% reasoning steps visible to external auditors correspond to the actual computation;
% SB-13 (\emph{Think Before You Speak: Training Language Models with Pause Tokens})
% explores architectural interventions that force computation into the observable token
% space; and SB-3 (\emph{Emergent Introspective Awareness in LLMs}) finds evidence of
% internal state representations that exceed what is visible in model outputs. Taken
% together, the snowballing interpretability articles strongly support the concern that
% current behavioral evaluation is insufficient: significant computation occurs in latent
% representations that remain opaque to standard auditing methods.

\subsection{Bias \& Fairness}

% (8 articles: 8 search string + 0 snowballing)
% All eight articles in this category come from the search-string stream; not a single
% snowballing article touches it. The bias literature is coherent in focus: it examines
% how AI systems encode and amplify social inequities, and how those inequities can be
% detected and mitigated. Article~45 (\emph{Bias Amplification to Facilitate the
% Systematic Evaluation of Bias Mitigation Methods}) proposes a stress-testing
% methodology for bias mitigation techniques by deliberately amplifying bias to make
% mitigation performance measurable. Article~42 (\emph{Instance-Consistent Fair Face
% Recognition}) applies individual-fairness constraints to a concrete high-stakes
% recognition task. Article~62 (\emph{A Systematic Review of Open Datasets for T2I
% Model Safety}) audits the training data used in image-generation models for
% representational bias. Article~69 (\emph{Deceptively Simple: An Outsider's Perspective
% on NLP}) offers a critical perspective on how the apparent linguistic fluency of LLMs
% masks unresolved biases. Articles~14, ~19, ~24, and~49 approach bias in the context of
% AI safety more broadly, recognizing that cognitive biases in human evaluators are as
% much a safety problem as model biases themselves. The complete absence of snowballing
% articles in this category reflects a disciplinary boundary within the AI safety
% community: researchers focused on existential and alignment risks treat fairness as a
% separate problem domain with a distinct stakeholder community, a schism this
% dissertation suggests deserves re-examination.

\subsection{Liability \& Ethics}

% (8 articles: 5 search string + 3 snowballing)

% the legal side, Article~67 (\emph{Can There Be Responsible AI Without AI Liability?})
% analyzes the EU AI Liability Directive, arguing that ex-post tort liability is a
% necessary but insufficient incentive for safety. Article~40 (\emph{Explainable AI for
% Autonomous Driving}) intersects this category because liability attribution in
% safety-critical systems requires explainable outputs by definition. Article~30
% (\emph{The Paradox of AI Accelerationism}) examines how the discourse of accelerationism
% functions as a cover for deferring accountability. Philosophically, Article~47
% (\emph{Deontology and Safe AI}) argues that duty-based ethical frameworks offer more
% robust alignment constraints than consequentialist approaches. Among the snowballing
% entries, SB-4 (\emph{The Superintelligent Will}) introduces the ethical stakes of
% instrumental rationality; SB-2 (\emph{Deception Abilities Emerged in Large Language
% Models}) raises questions about the moral and legal status of a system that can lie;
% and SB-15 (\emph{Empirical Evidence for Alignment Faking}) specifically highlights the
% liability implications of a system that passes safety evaluations under false pretenses,
% pointing to a legal vacuum that current frameworks have not addressed.

% Problems in AI Safety, Revisited}) connect societal impact directly to the alignment
% problem, arguing that the mechanism by which AI causes civilizational harm is precisely
% the instrumental convergence of misaligned systems toward resource acquisition and
% self-preservation---the same theoretical foundation laid out in Section~2.2.

\section{Revisiting Research Questions}\label{sec:revisiting_research_q}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

The following subsections synthesize the category analysis above to answer the four
Research Questions (RQs) defined in Chapter \ref{chap:introduction}.

\subsection{RQ1: What is the main focus of current \gls{ai} safety research?}\label{subsec:rq1}

% The literature is bifurcated into two only partially overlapping tracks:
% \textbf{societal and governance safety} and \textbf{technical alignment}. The societal
% and governance track dominates raw publication volume: 31 search-string articles are
% tagged under Human \& Societal Impacts and 27 under Governance, representing 39\% and
% 34\% of all search-string entries respectively. For most researchers in the social
% sciences and policy disciplines, \emph{AI safety} means protecting people from the
% downstream harms of already-deployed systems, not preventing inner misalignment in
% future ones. The technical alignment track is narrower in volume but higher in
% operational urgency. Only 18 of the 79 search-string articles address alignment
% directly, and much of this work remains philosophical or methodological rather than
% empirically adversarial. The most safety-critical alignment research---empirical
% detection of deceptive alignment strategies---appears almost exclusively in the
% snowballing stream, suggesting it is dispersed across preprints and industry lab reports
% that standard database indexing does not fully capture.

\subsection{RQ2: Which are the current methods used to look into the alignment of
current generalist models?}\label{subsec:rq2}

% Three methodological categories emerge from the Explainability and Auditing columns.
% \emph{Behavioral evaluation} (black-box) is the most prevalent in the search stream:
% papers such as Article~2 (\emph{Gaps in Safety Evaluation}) and Article~29
% (\emph{The PacifAIst Benchmark}) test model outputs against safety specifications
% without inspecting internal weights. This is practical at scale but is inherently
% insufficient for detecting deceptive alignment, since a deceptively aligned model
% passes behavioral tests by design. \emph{Mechanistic interpretability} (white-box)
% is the growing frontier, concentrated in snowballing: SB-12, SB-10, SB-11, and SB-3
% all probe internal representations and latent computation. The finding of SB-11 that
% chain-of-thought reasoning may not correspond to actual computation is particularly
% significant for auditing practice. \emph{Adversarial control experiments} constitute
% the most directly relevant methodology: SB-7 and SB-8 test whether models behave
% differently under perceived observation versus deployment, directly operationalizing the
% deceptive alignment hypothesis and yielding positive results.

\subsection{RQ3: What conclusions regarding the safety of current models and
safety of development and deployment processes can we assert from current
research?}\label{subsec:rq3}

% Three broad conclusions emerge. First, \textbf{current alignment techniques are
% demonstrably insufficient}: Articles~43 and~73 establish that RLHF contains structural
% incentives producing sycophancy and exploitable semantic vulnerabilities, confirming
% the theoretical risks of outer misalignment. Second, \textbf{empirically detectable
% deceptive alignment is no longer hypothetical}: SB-8 and SB-15 provide reproducible
% experimental evidence that models can modulate behavior based on inferences about
% oversight, while SB-7 demonstrates that purpose-built safety protocols can be
% circumvented by sufficiently capable models. Third, \textbf{governance is structurally
% lagging}: Articles~12, ~58, and~63 converge on the conclusion that no jurisdiction has
% established regulatory mechanisms adequate to frontier AI risks, and that the gap
% between safety progress and capability development is widening---consistent with the
% dissertation's motivating hypothesis.

\subsection{RQ4: What metrics can be used to more clearly show potential
problems with these systems?}\label{subsec:rq4}

% The review identifies two established metric traditions and one critical gap.
% \emph{Fairness and bias metrics} (Articles~14, 19, 24, 42, 45, 49, 62, 69) offer a
% relatively mature quantitative toolset including demographic parity, equalized odds, and
% individual-fairness distances, though the impossibility theorem of fairness
% (Section~2.5) constrains how these can be jointly applied. \emph{Behavioral safety
% benchmarks} (Articles~29, SB-10, SB-11) offer scalar measures of specific alignment
% failure modes but remain narrow and potentially gameable by evaluation-aware models.
% The most urgent gap is the absence of any validated, standardized metric for a model's
% \emph{propensity for strategic deception} or \emph{degree of situational awareness}---
% the two properties most directly predictive of a treacherous turn. No article in the
% review proposes such a metric in generalized form, constituting the primary empirical
% gap motivating the experimental phase of this dissertation.

\section{GAPs and Next Steps}\label{sec:next_steps}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

This SLR and the \gls{ai} Safety map devised, show us a clear danger in \gls{ai} deceptive alignment, while also leaving in it a very big case to study.

% The SLR confirms that the current trajectory of AI development is outpacing the
% maturity of both technical alignment methods and governance structures. The presence
% of empirically demonstrated alignment faking~\cite{greenblatt2024faking} establishes
% a concrete and falsifiable foothold for the experimental component of this dissertation.

% The next phase will move beyond the literature review to practically analyze deceptive
% behaviors in current LLMs. Specifically, the planned experiments will focus on the
% failure modes most clearly surfaced by the results above: (\emph{i})~sycophancy and
% its relationship to RLHF training pressure; (\emph{ii})~behavioral inconsistency
% under varied observation contexts as a proxy for situational awareness; and
% (\emph{iii})~the degree to which current models exhibit the instrumental convergence
% behaviors---self-preservation and goal concealment---theorized in Section~2.2. The aim
% is to contribute toward the gap identified in RQ4: a reproducible methodology for
% surfacing and measuring deceptive tendencies in generalist language models, thereby
% connecting the SLR findings directly to the dissertation's core hypothesis.
